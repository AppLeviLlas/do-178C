Understanding Requirements in DO-178C

Requirements are the cornerstone of aviation software development under DO-178C. They define what the software must do, how it must perform, and under what conditions it must operate. Well-defined, clear, and traceable requirements are essential for developing safe and compliant aviation software.

DO-178C places significant emphasis on requirements development, management, and verification. The standard defines different levels of requirements, from high-level requirements that capture system functionality to low-level requirements that guide implementation details.

The requirements process is not just about documentation—it's about establishing a solid foundation for the entire development lifecycle. Requirements drive design decisions, implementation strategies, and verification activities. They also serve as the basis for demonstrating compliance with certification objectives.

This page explores the key aspects of requirements in aviation software certification, including best practices for development, management, and verification under DO-178C.

Key Concepts

High-Level Requirements:
Derived from system requirements, define software functions
Low-Level Requirements:
Detailed software requirements derived from high-level requirements
Derived Requirements:
Requirements not directly traceable to higher-level requirements
Traceability:
Mapping between requirements at different levels
Verification:
Reviews, analyses, and tests to ensure requirements are met

Requirements Development

  • Completeness: Requirements must cover all intended functions and safety aspects.
  • Correctness: Requirements must accurately reflect the intended behavior.
  • Consistency: Requirements must not contradict each other.
  • Verifiability: Requirements must be stated in a way that allows verification.
  • Unambiguity: Requirements must have only one interpretation.
  • Modifiability: Requirements structure must facilitate changes.
  • Traceability: Requirements must be traceable to higher-level requirements.

Requirements Verification

  • Reviews: Systematic examination of requirements for quality attributes.
  • Traceability Analysis: Ensuring all requirements are traced to higher-level requirements.
  • Algorithm Analysis: Evaluating the correctness of algorithms specified in requirements.
  • Requirements-Based Testing: Testing designed to verify implementation meets requirements.
  • Independence: Verification activities performed by someone other than the author.
  • Coverage Analysis: Ensuring all requirements are verified by tests.

Common Requirements Challenges

  • Ambiguity: Requirements that can be interpreted in multiple ways.
  • Incompleteness: Missing requirements or aspects of functionality.
  • Inconsistency: Conflicting requirements within the specification.
  • Unverifiability: Requirements that cannot be objectively verified.
  • Scope Creep: Uncontrolled growth in requirements during development.
  • Traceability Gaps: Requirements not properly traced to higher levels.
  • Derived Requirements: Managing requirements not traceable to higher levels.

Best Practices

  • Use Formal Methods: Consider formal specification languages for critical requirements.
  • Implement Requirements Management Tools: Use tools to manage traceability and changes.
  • Establish Review Processes: Conduct regular reviews with diverse stakeholders.
  • Define Clear Attributes: Establish attributes for requirements (safety-critical, derived, etc.).
  • Document Rationale: Capture the reasoning behind requirements decisions.
  • Maintain Change History: Track changes to requirements over time.
  • Validate with Stakeholders: Ensure requirements meet stakeholder needs.
